LGBTQ+ Legal Rights: Separating Law from Fear

Being queer or trans is not illegal in the United States. It is not illegal in Utah. Start there. There's a lot of fear right now, and some of it is warranted. But fear without facts helps no one. This page collects actual legislation, actual executive orders, and actual bill text so you can assess your own risk. Where something is scary, it says so. Where it sounds scary but isn't law, it says that too.

Focus: United States Federal + Utah  |  Last updated March 2026

TL;DR
  • Being queer or trans is legal. No federal or Utah law criminalizes LGBTQ+ identity, relationships, or gender expression.
  • No sex offender registry for trans people. That claim comes from Project 2025's foreword. It is rhetoric in a policy document, not legislation. No bill has been introduced.
  • Most current damage is through executive orders, not laws. EOs can be reversed by a future president on day one. No anti-LGBTQ+ statute has passed both chambers of Congress.
  • The real risks in Utah right now: changing room restrictions in government buildings (HB 257), youth gender-affirming care moratorium (SB 16), and the federal trans military ban (EO 14183).
  • Adult gender-affirming care is unrestricted for self-pay and private insurance. The public funding ban (HB 193) failed.

01 Risk Assessment

The honest answer to "am I in danger?" depends on your specific situation. Here's the breakdown.

What is actually legal right now

  • ✓ Being gay, bisexual, queer, or trans
  • ✓ Same-sex relationships and marriage
  • ✓ Cross-dressing and gender-nonconforming appearance (First Amendment; no law in any state criminalizes how you dress)
  • ✓ Drag performances (protected expression; courts have struck down state-level restrictions)
  • ✓ Using your chosen name socially, or changing it legally through a court order
  • ✓ Gender-affirming care as an adult, self-pay (no restrictions anywhere)
  • ✓ Gender-affirming care as an adult, private insurance (coverage varies by plan, but no law bans it in Utah)
  • ✓ Changing gender markers on Utah birth certificates and driver's licenses (HB 234, still in effect)

What carries actual legal risk right now

ActionRisk LevelDetails
Serving openly as trans in the U.S. military Discharge EO 14183. Currently in effect after SCOTUS ruling (May 2025). Not theoretical.
Using a gov't changing room that doesn't match birth sex (Utah) Class B Misdemeanor HB 257. Only changing rooms (not stand-alone restrooms). Only gov't buildings. Surgical exemption exists. Max 6 months.
Providing gender-affirming hormones/blockers to minors (Utah) Professional Sanctions SB 16 moratorium. HB 174 would make it permanent.
Applying for a federal passport with a non-birth-sex marker Denied EO 14168. X markers and self-identified gender no longer accepted on passports/visas.
ℹ Executive orders are not permanent law
  • Every anti-LGBTQ+ action at the federal level has been through executive orders, not legislation. EOs can be reversed by a future president on day one.
  • No anti-LGBTQ+ statute has passed both chambers of Congress. The trans sports ban passed the House but was defeated in the Senate.
  • The legal infrastructure protecting LGBTQ+ rights (Bostock v. Clayton County, Obergefell v. Hodges, the Respect for Marriage Act) remains intact and binding.

Voting with a mismatched name or gender marker

ℹ Your vote is safe
  • No law (federal or Utah) requires your gender marker to match your voter registration.
  • Utah uses signature verification, not gender matching, to validate ballots.
  • If you've legally changed your name, update your voter registration to match. A name mismatch can trigger a signature flag (curable, but annoying).
  • A gender marker mismatch alone will not prevent you from voting or invalidate your ballot.
  • If you're worried about the SAVE Act's impact on voting, see the full SAVE Act guide.

What is NOT law despite the fear

✓ Reality check
  • Being put on a sex offender registry for being trans: Not law. Project 2025 rhetoric targets educators/librarians who distribute material they define as pornographic. No bill introduced. See the actual quote below.
  • Nationwide criminalization of being trans: Not happening. No such bill exists.
  • Criminalizing cross-dressing or drag: Not law. The First Amendment protects gender expression and artistic performance. Courts have struck down attempts in other states.
  • Banning adult gender-affirming care: Not law federally. Utah's HB 193 (public funding ban) failed. Private and self-pay care unrestricted.
  • Forced detransition: Not law. HB 174 targets minors only.
  • Revoking same-sex marriage: Not law. Obergefell + Respect for Marriage Act. No active legislation with realistic chances.
  • Losing your right to vote because of a gender marker mismatch: Not how it works. See above.

What you should actually pay attention to

1

If you're in the military: The trans ban is in effect. This is the highest-severity, most immediately consequential item on this list.

2

If you're trans in Utah and use government rec centers, school facilities, or similar: HB 257 is real. Know which spaces are changing rooms vs. restrooms. The law makes a legal distinction. Stand-alone restrooms have no criminal penalty.

3

If you're a trans minor or parent of one in Utah: The care moratorium is law, and HB 174 would make it permanent. If your child is receiving treatment under grandfathering provisions, that access may end.

4

If you need to update federal documents: Passport gender markers are locked to birth sex under EO 14168. Utah state documents (DL, birth certificate) are still changeable, but HB 183 could end that. If you're planning to update state docs, sooner may be better than later.

5

If you're trans and not a U.S. citizen: Document mismatches are now a real risk. USCIS only recognizes birth sex on immigration forms. Consular officers can request proof of birth sex and deny visas over mismatches, and those discrepancies feed into ICE enforcement databases. Even if you've legally changed your gender marker on a state ID, your federal immigration records may not match. Get legal advice from Immigration Equality before changing any documents or traveling internationally.

6

If you rely on Medicaid or state employee insurance for gender-affirming care: HB 193 failed this session, but the political will to cut funding isn't going away. Private insurance and self-pay are safe for now. Consider backup plans for public-funded care.

7

If HB 183 is reintroduced and passes: It died in committee this session, but could come back. It would be the most significant single change for trans Utahns: losing employment/housing protections, ability to change gender markers, and eligibility to work with children.

8

If you're trans and could face incarceration: Federal prisons now house inmates by birth sex under EO 14168, regardless of transition status. Access to hormones in federal custody is being restricted. State and county jails in Utah have no consistent policy on providing gender-affirming care or respecting gender identity in housing. If you're on hormones, abrupt cessation during incarceration is a real medical risk. The ACLU has active litigation on this.

9

If you've changed your legal name: Update your voter registration, driver's license, and bank accounts to match. Mismatched names can cause bureaucratic friction (signature verification issues on ballots, ID checks, etc.). This isn't a legal risk per se, but it's a practical one.

Protect yourself now

  • ☐ Update your voter registration if you've changed your legal name
  • ☐ Save copies of your current gender-marker documents (birth certificate, driver's license)
  • ☐ Save the ACLU of Utah's contact info: acluutah.org
  • ☐ If you're on Medicaid or state employee insurance for gender-affirming care, look into private insurance options as a backup
⚠ Should you update your gender marker?

Utah's HB 234 still allows gender marker changes on birth certificates and driver's licenses. HB 183 could end that if reintroduced. But rushing to change isn't always the right move.

Risk of changing: In Kansas, SB 244 (Feb 2026) retroactively invalidated the driver's licenses of ~1,700 trans residents who had already changed their markers, with no grace period. If Utah passes something similar, an updated marker could become a liability rather than a protection.

Risk for non-citizens: Mismatched gender markers across documents (passport, visa, green card, DL) can trigger fraud investigations, visa denials, and ICE scrutiny. USCIS now only recognizes birth sex on immigration forms. Consular officers can request proof of birth sex and deny visas over mismatches. Immigration attorneys warn these discrepancies feed into enforcement databases.

Bottom line: There's no clearly safe choice. If you're a U.S. citizen, weigh whether the benefit of matching documents outweighs the risk of a rollback. If you're not a U.S. citizen, get legal advice from Immigration Equality before changing any documents.

04 Project 2025

The Heritage Foundation's Mandate for Leadership is a policy blueprint. It is not law. But portions have been implemented through executive orders, and it reveals the ideology driving current legislation.

The Sex Offender Claim

This is the passage generating the most fear. It's from the Mandate's foreword (page 5), written by Heritage Foundation president Kevin Roberts:

Project 2025, Mandate for Leadership, Foreword, p. 5
"Pornography, manifested today in the omnipresent propagation of transgender ideology and sexualization of children, for instance, is not a political Gordian knot inextricably binding up disparate claims about free speech, property rights, sexual liberation, and child welfare. It has no claim to First Amendment protection. Its purveyors are child predators and misogynistic exploiters of women. Their product is as addictive as any illicit drug and as psychologically destructive as any crime. Pornography should be outlawed. The people who produce and distribute it should be imprisoned. Educators and public librarians who purvey it should be classed as registered sex offenders."
⚠ What this actually means
  • The document equates "transgender ideology" with pornography. That's the rhetorical bridge used to classify LGBTQ+ visibility as obscenity.
  • The sex offender registry language targets educators and librarians who distribute material they define as pornographic, not trans people for existing. But the framing is designed to criminalize visibility by association.
  • None of this is law. No bill has passed (federal or state) that puts trans or queer people on sex offender registries for their identity.
  • The danger is normalization. When policy documents equate queerness with predation, it creates a permission structure for future legislation.

05 Federal Executive Orders

These are in effect now. Executive orders carry the force of policy until overturned by courts or a future president.

EO 14168 In Effect
"Defending Women from Gender Ideology Extremism and Restoring Biological Truth to the Federal Government," January 20, 2025
Executive Order 14168, Section 2
"It is the policy of the United States to recognize two sexes, male and female. These sexes are not changeable and are grounded in fundamental and incontrovertible reality. [...] 'Sex' is not a synonym for and does not include the concept of 'gender identity.'"
What it does
  • All federal agencies must replace "gender" with "sex" in materials and forms
  • Passports, visas, and federal IDs must reflect birth sex only; no X markers
  • Agencies directed to cease funding gender-affirming care for people under 19
  • Federal prisons must house inmates by birth sex (affects 2,000+ trans inmates)
  • Federal funding withheld from programs promoting "gender ideology"
Legal challenges

Multiple provisions challenged in court. Temporary restraining orders issued against funding withholding and forced prison transfers. Passport/ID provisions are being implemented.

EO 14183 In Effect (SCOTUS)
"Prioritizing Military Excellence and Readiness" / Transgender Military Ban, January 27, 2025
Executive Order 14183
"Gender dysphoria is incompatible with the demands of military service."
Timeline
  • Jan 27, 2025: Order signed
  • Mar 2025: Federal judge blocked the ban
  • May 2025: Supreme Court allowed the ban to proceed while appeals continue
  • ~1,000 service members began voluntary separation
  • The ban is currently in effect

06 Federal Legislation

As of March 2026, 113 bills targeting LGBTQ+ people are being tracked at the federal level (119th Congress). Over 850 anti-LGBTQ+ bills were filed in 2025 alone, the most in U.S. history.

Nationwide Trans Youth Sports Ban Defeated

Passed the House but was defeated in the Senate. Would have banned trans girls from school sports nationally.

SAVE America Act (H.R. 7296) In Senate

Primarily a voter ID bill, but Trump insisted on anti-trans amendments (sports ban, care ban) as conditions for signing. Passed House; expected to fail Senate filibuster. See full breakdown →

Transgender Bill of Rights (H.Res.1058) Introduced

Introduced Feb 11, 2026 by Rep. Pramila Jayapal (D-WA) with 90+ co-sponsors, companion resolution by Sen. Ed Markey (D-MA). A non-binding resolution calling on the federal government to implement a comprehensive framework of protections. No chance of passing the current Congress, but puts specific provisions on record. Referred to Judiciary, Education, Energy and Commerce, Financial Services, Armed Services, Veterans' Affairs, and other committees.

What it calls for
  • Public accommodations and education: amend the Civil Rights Act to ban discrimination based on gender identity and sex characteristics in public accommodations and federally funded programs; protect students from discrimination in classes, athletics, and facilities
  • Healthcare and bodily autonomy: eliminate government restrictions on gender-affirming care for adults and adolescents; protect providers from criminal/civil penalties for following recognized standards of care; ban nonconsensual surgeries on intersex children; ban conversion practices; reopen the NIH Sexual & Gender Minority Research Office
  • Employment, housing, and credit: codify Bostock by amending Title VII, the Fair Housing Act, and the Equal Credit Opportunity Act to explicitly cover gender identity and sex characteristics
  • Identity documents: eliminate barriers to updating sex and names on passports, Social Security cards, and federal IDs (self-attestation where possible); require X marker availability; allow same-day voter registration name/gender updates
  • Military: ensure trans servicemembers can serve openly; require TRICARE and VA to cover gender-affirming care; review and reclassify discharges of trans veterans
  • Immigration: eliminate anti-trans bias in asylum proceedings; require culturally competent training for adjudicators; protect gender identity as grounds for asylum claims
  • Safety and incarceration: housing assignments in government custody based on safety needs, not birth sex; ban involuntary solitary confinement based on gender identity; ensure access to gender-affirming care in jails, prisons, and immigration detention
  • Enforcement: dedicated DOJ Civil Rights Division liaison for trans rights; appropriations for enforcement across agencies

The resolution notes that gender-affirming care is endorsed by the AMA, AAP, APA, the Endocrine Society, and 15+ other major medical organizations.

ℹ Bottom line on federal law
  • No new federal statute criminalizing LGBTQ+ identity or conduct has been enacted.
  • The damage is through executive orders, agency policy, and funding threats; not legislation that passed both chambers.
  • Executive orders can be reversed by a future president. Statutes are harder to undo.

07 Utah: Laws in Effect

These are signed into law and enforceable today.

HB 257 Law, Jan 2024
Sex-based Designations for Privacy, Anti-bullying and Women's Opportunities

This is the law most likely to directly affect trans Utahns day-to-day. The details matter; it's narrower than most people think, but the penalties are real.

What's restricted

Sex-designated changing rooms (locker rooms, communal showers, fitting rooms) in government-owned buildings: schools, courthouses, libraries, rec centers, airports, state-owned arenas.

What's NOT restricted
  • Stand-alone restrooms in government buildings (no criminal penalty)
  • Anything in privately owned buildings (restaurants, malls, hotels, private gyms)
  • Employee-only areas
  • Unisex / single-occupant facilities
Penalties
ViolationChargeMax Penalty
Entering a gov't changing room that doesn't match birth sex Class B misdemeanor 6 months jail
Same, while also committing lewdness/voyeurism Class A misdemeanor 1 year jail
Same, with aggravating conduct Third-degree felony 5 years prison

Note: lewdness and voyeurism are already crimes for everyone. The elevated penalties apply when committed in a facility that doesn't match your birth sex.

Exemptions
  • People who've had primary sex characteristics surgery and updated their birth certificate
  • Intersex individuals
  • Minor children accompanied by parents
  • Dependent adults with caretakers
  • Public safety, healthcare, and maintenance personnel
How it's enforced

Only law enforcement can investigate. Random citizens cannot demand your ID. The State Auditor investigates whether government entities are complying (posting signs, making accommodations), not policing individual bathroom users. No documentation is legally required to use a public restroom.

SB 16 Law, Jan 2023
Youth Gender-Affirming Care Moratorium

Placed a moratorium on puberty blockers and cross-sex hormones for minors. Existing patients could continue under certain conditions.

ℹ The state's own study contradicts the ban
  • The legislature commissioned a medical evidence review under this bill.
  • It concluded gender-affirming care for minors with gender dysphoria "is largely found to result in positive outcomes and reduce the likelihood of suicide."
  • HB 174 (2026) would make the ban permanent.
HB 234 Law, May 2024
Gender Marker Changes on Birth Certificates
  • Minors must be at least 15.5 years old to petition; change takes effect at 16
  • Amended certificates won't show they were amended (unless you request it)
  • State keeps internal records of changes
  • Name changes can be bundled with gender marker petitions

Still in effect but would be effectively nullified by HB 183 if it passes.

08 Utah: 2026 Session Bills

The fifth consecutive year of anti-trans legislation. Status as of early March 2026.

HB 174 Passed House → Senate
Permanent Youth Gender-Affirming Care Ban

Converts the 2023 moratorium into a permanent ban. Passed House 54–15. Requires providers to taper existing patients off treatment. The ACLU of Utah sent a veto letter to Governor Cox.

Why it matters

The state's own commissioned study found positive outcomes and reduced suicide risk. The legislature is advancing the ban despite the study's findings.

HB 193 Failed
Public Funding Ban for Gender-Affirming Care (All Ages)

Would have banned Medicaid, state employee insurance, and all public funds from covering gender-affirming procedures or hormones for anyone. Failed a motion to recommend.

It failed, but it signals the direction. If you're on state insurance or Medicaid, coverage remains politically vulnerable.

HB 183 Died in Committee
Sex Designation Amendments ("The Big One")

Was sitting in the House Rules Committee when the session adjourned sine die. Did not pass. Could be reintroduced in a future session. This was the most sweeping anti-trans bill Utah has seen.

What it would do
  • Replace "gender" with "sex" throughout Utah state law
  • Remove anti-discrimination protections for trans people in housing and employment
  • Effectively ban gender marker changes on birth certificates
  • Bar trans people from working with children in public schools and certain healthcare settings (if they "do not present as their biological sex" and the role involves contact with a child for more than 5 minutes). Note: Bostock v. Clayton County (2020) established that Title VII prohibits employment discrimination based on transgender status. Similar provisions in other states have been blocked by courts on equal protection grounds.
  • In custody disputes, courts must rule in favor of the parent who opposes a child's gender identity
HB 95 Died in Committee
Misgendering Protection for Government Employees

Prevents disciplinary action against public school teachers or government employees who refuse to use a student's or coworker's requested name/pronouns, as long as it's done "in good faith" or based on "sincerely held religious or moral belief."

HB 404 Passed House → Senate
Student Housing Discrimination

Extends sex-based housing segregation rules from university-run dormitories to private dormitories and single-sex spaces.

HB 175 Died in Committee
Pride Event Funding Ban

Prohibits state-funded businesses from hosting Pride-themed events.

HB 196 & HB 302 Died in Committee
Harvey Milk Blvd Renaming / Pride Flag Restrictions

HB 196 would rename Harvey Milk Boulevard in SLC for Charlie Kirk. HB 302 limits cities to one official flag, letting the legislature review municipal flag adoptions.
